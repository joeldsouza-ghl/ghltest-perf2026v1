Philosophy & Guiding Principles

The foundation that drives how we evaluate, grow, and recognize performance at HighLevel

💡 Performance Philosophy

HighLevel's performance management process is designed to help employees understand where they have made impact and where they can grow. It enables managers to provide clear, actionable feedback and development guidance, connects individual contributions to team and company goals, and grounds performance outcomes in clarity, consistency, and fairness.

🧭 Five Guiding Principles

1. Performance = Outcomes + Behaviours — You are assessed on what you deliver and how you deliver it. Both matter equally.
2. Customer-Centricity — Creating customer value is the north star and key evaluation lens for all roles.
3. Bias for Action — We recognize ownership, speed, experimentation, and decisiveness.
4. Growth-Mindedness — Every employee should receive meaningful developmental input. We encourage learning from failures.
5. Ongoing Feedback — Reviews reflect year-round conversations and feedback, not just a once-a-year snapshot.

📖 Detailed Rating Definitions

Exceeds High Expectations ▼

Definition: Regularly goes beyond expectations, delivering high-quality work and strong impact.

What this looks like:

  • Consistently delivers outcomes that are above and beyond role requirements
  • Takes ownership of initiatives, drives innovation, and mentors others
  • Proactively identifies problems and implements solutions before being asked
  • Demonstrates all core behaviors at a high level across the entire review period

Example (Tech): Led adoption of a key product feature that increased user engagement by 30%, mentored two junior developers, and proactively redesigned test automation that reduced regression time by 40%.

Meets High Expectations ▼

Definition: Consistently fulfills role responsibilities and delivers expected results.

What this looks like:

  • Reliably meets all role expectations with quality output
  • Demonstrates solid competency in technical and behavioral areas
  • Handles responsibilities independently and collaborates effectively
  • Shows growth and learning throughout the review period

Example (Customer Success): Maintained CSAT above 90%, resolved escalations within SLA, contributed to knowledge base documentation, and actively participated in team initiatives.

Sometimes Meets High Expectations ▼

Definition: Inconsistently meets expectations; requires development in key areas and additional support.

What this looks like:

  • Delivers results that are uneven — strong in some areas, falling short in others
  • May need reminders or additional guidance to meet deadlines or quality standards
  • Shows potential but hasn't yet demonstrated consistency
  • Development areas have been identified but progress is partial
Does Not Meet High Expectations ▼

Definition: Performance does not meet expectations; requires continued effort and support to improve.

What this looks like:

  • Consistently falls short of role requirements despite feedback and support
  • Limited accountability for outcomes and limited response to coaching
  • Quality or productivity significantly below expected levels
  • May require a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)

👤 Employee Guide

Everything you need to navigate your performance review with confidence

📝 Your Self-Review: 3 Questions to Answer

Think of your self-review as your opportunity to tell your story — what you achieved, how you grew, and where you want to go.

Q1: Key Contributions, Achievements & Wins

Provide examples with data. Think about outcomes you delivered and how you delivered them.

Strong example: "Led the redesign of the onboarding flow, increasing trial-to-paid conversion by 18%. Collaborated with 3 cross-functional teams and delivered 2 weeks ahead of schedule."

Weak example: "Worked on onboarding stuff and did a good job."

Q2: Areas for Improvement & Support Needed

Be honest about where you can grow and specific about what support would help.

Strong example: "I want to improve my ability to influence cross-functional stakeholders. Having a mentor from the product leadership team would help me develop this skill."

Weak example: "I could be better at communication."

Q3: Career Aspirations & Growth

Share where you see yourself heading and what the organization can do to help you get there.

🎯 Tips for a Great Self-Review

Reflect ahead of time — Don't wait until the form opens. Start noting your wins throughout the year.
Use the formula — Outcomes (What) + Behaviours (How) = Impact. Frame every example this way.
Bring the data — Numbers, metrics, and specific examples make your story compelling.
Own your growth — Be specific about development areas and the support you need.

🤝 Peer Review: What to Know

Your manager will nominate peers to provide feedback on your performance. Here's what peer reviewers are asked:

Question 1: What has [Name] done particularly well this year?
Question 2: What areas could [Name] improve or develop further?
Peer feedback is confidential — visible only to your manager and respective SLT. Use this knowledge to be authentic and constructive when giving peer feedback to others.

💬 How to Navigate the Review Conversation

  • Listen with openness — View feedback as input, not judgment
  • Make it two-way — Ask questions, share your perspective, discuss development
  • Focus forward — Use the conversation to align on next-cycle goals and growth
  • Follow up — Document key takeaways and your development commitments

🏛️ Leader / SLT Guide

Strategic oversight of calibration, promotions, and organizational performance health

⚖️ Three-Stage Calibration Process

Calibration ensures consistency, reduces bias, and validates ratings across the organization. It is grounded in role expectations, not peer comparisons.

Stage 1: Functional Review
Who: Director + PBP + Line Managers
Focus: Rating alignment to role expectations, justification quality, outlier identification, evidence sufficiency
Purpose: Ensure consistency and reduce bias within functional teams
Stage 2: Departmental Review
Who: Director + HOD/SLT + Optional Cross-Functional
Focus: Consolidated ratings and promotions across function; shared understanding of level expectations
Note: L3→L4+ promotions require joint discussion with SLT leader, relevant Directors, and Sr. Managers
Stage 3: Final Review
Who: SLT Leader + Co-Founder + CPO
Focus: Executive-level validation of finalized ratings and promotions
Outcome: PBP obtains CPO approval; ratings finalized in system

⚖️ Calibration Deep Dive

Ensuring consistency, fairness, and transparency across the organization

Why Calibration Matters

Calibration is the critical step ensuring consistency, reducing bias, and validating ratings across the organization. It is grounded in role expectations — not peer comparisons — and uses evidence from performance data, peer feedback, and manager insight.

Three Calibration Stages

1
Functional Review
Director + PBP + Managers
2
Departmental Review
Director + HOD/SLT
3
Final Review
SLT + Co-Founder + CPO

Stage 1: Functional Review

Each Director meets with PBP and line managers to ensure ratings are consistent within their function. Focus areas: justification quality, rating definition alignment, outlier identification, and evidence sufficiency.

Stage 2: Departmental Review

Each Director presents consolidated ratings and promotions to their HOD/SLT leader. In-depth review to build shared understanding of level expectations. Cross-functional calibration for P5/M4+ is encouraged.

Stage 3: Final Review

Each SLT leader reviews finalized ratings and promotions with the Co-Founder and CPO for executive-level validation. PBP obtains CPO approval of the validated ratings and promotion approvals.

🎯 Rewards Philosophy & Principles

The guiding framework behind every compensation decision at HighLevel

Philosophy

HighLevel's rewards strategy is designed to attract, motivate, and retain exceptional talent globally. It fosters ownership, supports holistic well-being, and reinforces a culture where meaningful impact and accelerated growth are recognized and celebrated.

5 Design Principles

1. Total Rewards Mindset — Compensation, benefits, well-being, and recognition form a dynamic ecosystem supporting performance and growth.
2. Market Competitive — Competitive pay and benefits across all markets. Elevated positioning for high-impact and scarce-skill roles (AI/ML, Engineering, Leadership).
3. Pay for Performance — Rewards measurable outcomes aligned with individual, team, and company performance.
4. Simplicity & Transparency — Employees understand how pay is determined and can influence it through their performance.
5. Benefits as Strategic Lever — Supporting holistic well-being (physical, mental, financial) for sustained high performance.

👤 How do I write a compelling self-review? ▼

Use the Outcomes + Behaviours = Impact formula for every point. Start by listing 3-5 key contributions with specific metrics. For example, instead of "I improved the product," write "Led the checkout redesign that increased conversion by 18%, collaborating with 3 cross-functional teams."

Tips: Reflect before writing. Gather data throughout the year. Include both metric-driven wins and effort-based contributions (mentorship, knowledge transfer). Be honest about areas for growth — it shows self-awareness.

👤 I disagree with my performance rating. What should I do? ▼

First, approach the conversation with openness. Ask your manager to walk you through the specific evidence that informed the rating. Share your perspective with concrete examples. If you still feel the rating doesn't reflect your contributions, you can ask your manager to discuss it with their PBP. Remember that ratings go through 3 levels of calibration, so they reflect input from multiple leaders.

Key mindset shift: Treat feedback as data, not judgment. Focus the conversation on what you can do differently going forward, and ask for specific development support.

👤 How should I prepare for my review conversation? ▼

Come prepared with your self-review points fresh in mind. Think about questions you want to ask: What went well? Where can I grow? What does success look like next year? Be ready to listen openly, take notes, and treat the conversation as two-way. Follow up by documenting your key takeaways and development commitments.

👥 How do I rate a borderline performer? ▼

Evaluate the full review period — not just recent performance. Use the What + How + Impact lens. Ask: "Were outcomes consistently delivered? Were behaviors aligned to expectations most of the time?" If they met expectations in some areas but fell short in others, "Sometimes Meets" may be appropriate. Document specific examples for both strengths and gaps to support your rating in calibration.

Common pitfall: Avoid defaulting to "Meets" to avoid a difficult conversation. Clear differentiation helps the employee get the development support they need.

👥 How do I deliver tough feedback constructively? ▼

Lead with specific observations, not generalizations. Use "I observed..." rather than "You always..." Frame development areas as growth opportunities. For example: "I noticed the Q3 project deliverables were delayed by 2 weeks. Let's discuss what support would help you manage timelines more effectively." Always pair development feedback with clear next steps and your commitment to support their growth.

👥 When should I nominate someone for promotion? ▼

Nominate when the employee has been in role 12+ months, demonstrates sustained high performance, and is already operating at the next level in scope and complexity. The strongest cases show: measurable impact on business outcomes, initiative beyond current scope (coaching, team processes, hiring support), and peer/stakeholder feedback supporting the promotion. Remember to prepare a solid business case using the promotion nomination template.

🏛️ How do I run an effective calibration session? ▼

Start by aligning on rating definitions — ensure everyone in the room has the same understanding of each level. Review proposed ratings against role expectations (not peer comparisons). Focus discussion on outliers and borderline cases. Ask managers to present evidence, not opinions. Watch for recency bias, central tendency, and halo effects. End by confirming all ratings have sufficient justification and document any adjustments made.

🏛️ What if my team's distribution doesn't match the guidelines? ▼

The distribution guidelines (20/50/20/10) are directional benchmarks, not forced distributions. If your team genuinely has more high performers (or more development needs), be prepared to justify the distribution with specific evidence during calibration. The key is that every rating is substantiated and defensible. Come prepared with concrete examples for each employee, especially those in the "Exceeds" or "Does Not Meet" categories.

🏛️ How do I handle disagreements between managers during calibration? ▼

Redirect the conversation to evidence and role expectations. Ask: "What specific outcomes and behaviors are we evaluating?" Ensure both perspectives are heard, then align on what the rating definitions actually require. Use the PBP as a neutral facilitator. If consensus can't be reached, escalate to the next calibration level with both viewpoints documented.

Situation
Your self-review feels overwhelming

Start with just your top 3 wins. For each, write one sentence about what you did, one about how you did it, and one about the impact. Then expand from there. Use the Outcomes + Behaviours formula. Don't try to capture everything — focus on the most meaningful contributions.

Conversation Starter
Asking your manager for growth support

Try: "I'd like to develop my skills in [area]. Could we discuss what projects or mentorship opportunities might help me grow in that direction over the next cycle?"

Development
You received unexpected critical feedback

Take a breath. Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me a specific example?" and "What would good look like going forward?" Write down the feedback and revisit it in 24 hours. Use it to build a concrete development plan. Remember: feedback is data for growth.

Situation
An employee is defensive about their rating

Acknowledge their feelings first: "I understand this is not what you expected." Then walk through the specific evidence calmly. Ask: "What's your perspective on [specific example]?" Focus on the forward path: what needs to happen for a different outcome next cycle. Avoid debating — share evidence and listen.

Conversation Starter
Opening the review conversation

Try: "I appreciate the time you put into your self-review. I want this to be a two-way conversation — let me share my observations and then I'd love to hear your perspective. My goal is for us to align on your strengths, growth areas, and what success looks like going forward."

Development
Supporting a high performer who wants promotion

Be transparent about criteria: 12+ months in role, sustained performance, next-level evidence. Help them build their case by identifying stretch projects. If they're not ready yet, say: "Here's what I see as the gap, and here's how we can close it together over the next 6 months."

Situation
A manager is inflating ratings for their team

Ask for specific evidence for each rating. Use the distribution guidelines as a conversation tool: "Your team has 80% in Exceeds — help me understand what's different about this team versus others." Compare evidence quality against the rating definitions. Partner with the PBP to coach the manager on differentiation.

Conversation Starter
Opening calibration with your directors

Try: "Let's start by aligning on what each rating level means for our teams. I want us to focus on evidence and role expectations — not comparisons between people. Where should we spend the most time today?"

Development
Building a stronger feedback culture

Model it yourself — give regular, specific feedback to your directs. Encourage managers to use continuous feedback in Darwinbox throughout the year, not just at review time. Recognize teams that demonstrate strong feedback practices. Make it safe to share development areas openly.

Think about: Where have you felt stretched? What skills do you wish you had more of? What feedback have you received throughout the year? Be specific — "I want to improve cross-functional stakeholder management" is better than "I want to communicate better." Then add what support would help: a mentor, a stretch project, a training program.

I just joined recently — how do I write a meaningful self-review? ▼

Focus on your onboarding contributions: How quickly did you ramp up? What early impact have you had? What have you learned? If eligible (hired by December cutoff), even a few months of contributions matter. Highlight early wins, learning velocity, and collaboration with your team.

How should I think about career aspirations? ▼

Be honest about what energizes you. Do you want to go deeper technically or move into leadership? Are there specific teams, projects, or skills you want to explore? This isn't a commitment — it's a signal to help the organization support your growth. The more specific you are, the more useful the conversation will be.

I gave great peer feedback but got a lower rating than expected ▼

Remember: peer feedback is one input among several. Your rating reflects the full picture — self-review, peer feedback, manager assessment, and calibration across the organization. Ask your manager to help you understand which specific areas were weighed in the final decision. Use this as input for your growth plan.

👥 Manager Situations

Handling tricky review scenarios with confidence

My employee did great work but their impact is hard to quantify ▼

Not all impact is metric-driven. Highlight effort-based contributions: knowledge transfer, mentoring, team morale, unblocking others, process improvements. Use phrases like "enabled the team to..." or "their support on X freed up the team to deliver Y." Peer feedback often captures this well — leverage those inputs.

Two employees have similar output but different behaviors ▼

This is exactly why Performance = Outcomes + Behaviours. If one employee delivers results but undermines collaboration, while the other delivers the same results and lifts the team up — that's a meaningful difference. Document the behavioral evidence and use it to differentiate ratings. The "How" matters as much as the "What."

My rating was changed during calibration and I need to explain it ▼

Be transparent: "Your rating was reviewed as part of our calibration process, where we ensure consistency across teams and functions." Share the evidence that informed the final decision. Avoid blaming the process — own the conversation. Focus on the forward-looking development plan.

An employee wants to know what peers said about them ▼

Peer feedback is confidential — visible only to the manager and SLT. You can share themes and synthesized insights without attributing specific comments. For example: "Multiple stakeholders highlighted your strength in X" or "Feedback suggests an opportunity to develop Y." Never reveal who said what.

🏛️ Leader Situations

Strategic performance management challenges

Multiple managers are requesting promotion for the same level — how do I prioritize? ▼

Return to the promotion criteria: time in role, performance consistency, next-level evidence, business alignment. Compare cases using the nomination template. Ask: "Who has the strongest evidence of already operating at the next level?" Consider organizational needs and role availability. Use calibration to surface the strongest cases and document why others may need more time.

I'm seeing recency bias across my org during calibration ▼

Name it openly in the calibration session. Ask managers to provide evidence from the full review period — not just the last quarter. Encourage the use of continuous feedback notes in Darwinbox year-round to combat this. You might ask: "What was this person's performance like in Q1 and Q2?" to broaden the lens.

How do I ensure compensation decisions are fair across my function? ▼

Work with your PBP and the Rewards team during the compensation planning phase. Review compa-ratios across your team. Ensure merit increases and equity refreshers are aligned to performance ratings. Flag any outliers where compensation doesn't match performance outcomes. The goal is for an ideal compa-ratio landing above 0.7 of the new level for promotions.
